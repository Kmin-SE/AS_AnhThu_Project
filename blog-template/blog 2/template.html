The Plastic Trap: Convenience at a Cost

< Back

At some American universities like Cornell University, the use of single-use plastic bottles remains common, even though the campus tap water meets safety standards and is drinkable. This reflects a general reality: many people still do not trust tap water and choose bottled water for convenience, despite the significant environmental impact of this choice.

Since the early 2000s, a student-led campaign called “Take Back the Tap” was launched to encourage reducing plastic bottle use and promote tap water consumption. The campaign focuses on raising awareness about the safety of tap water while publicly sharing water quality reports so people can easily access information.

Studies and lectures in environmental communication at Cornell indicate that bottled water often originates from public water systems, which is then filtered and bottled. Meanwhile, plastic bottles have a very high carbon footprint because they are produced from petroleum products and most cannot be recycled effectively. The overuse of bottled water not only causes environmental pollution but also contributes to the increasing volume of plastic waste.

Furthermore, the issue of clean water is linked to social and economic injustice. In some low-income communities where tap water is unsafe to drink, bottled water is a short-term necessity. However, a long-term reliance on this solution may lead society to neglect improving water supply systems, widening the gap in access to clean water.

It is concerning that the habit of using plastic bottles has become "normalized" even in places where tap water is perfectly safe, such as many university campuses. Student surveys show that the majority are unaware or do not believe that the school's tap water is high quality, sometimes even better than bottled water.

To change this, communication activities such as infographics, posters, stickers, and classroom discussions have been implemented to spread the message: drinking tap water is not only safe but also a sustainable choice for the environment. Some campus events have begun replacing plastic bottles with metal bottles and paper packaging, showing positive initial shifts.

However, the use of single-use plastic bottles remains widespread. Therefore, the goal of campaigns like "Take Back the Tap" is not just to change individual habits, but to drive systemic change so that everyone can access greener choices without feeling pressure or guilt.
