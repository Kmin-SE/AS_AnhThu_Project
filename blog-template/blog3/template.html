Breathing New Life into Old Plastic

< Back

Polystyrene—the material found in foam cups and takeout containers—is one of the most problematic forms of waste. It is virtually non-biodegradable and accounts for nearly one-third of global landfill volume. In the environment, it can leach toxic substances like styrene and benzene, often drifting into our oceans to become a pervasive form of marine litter.

To tackle this challenge, Stache Lab at Cornell University has developed a groundbreaking method to break down polystyrene simply and cost-effectively. By combining white light, oxygen from the air, and a small amount of iron-based catalyst, the research team can transform foam plastic into benzoic acid—a valuable chemical used widely in industrial applications.

Unlike traditional recycling, this process doesn't require extreme heat or complex machinery; it can even be powered by natural sunlight. Experiments show that most common types of polystyrene decompose efficiently, with the exception of black plastics (as the dark dye blocks the necessary light).

This research opens a new frontier in recycling hard-to-treat plastics, helping us turn trash into a resource and significantly reducing the global burden of pollution.
